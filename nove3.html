The Letter Only God Should Hear

While walking out of the office, her eyes were empty. The door closed behind her, and she stood there for a moment, as if waiting for something to break inside her — but nothing did. The City Center was loud, alive, indifferent. People walked past her with purpose. She felt like a ghost among the living.

The huge church stood tall across the square, unmoved by human chaos. She walked toward it slowly, as if walking toward a verdict already decided.

Inside, the silence pressed against her ears. She sat on the first bench.

She sat on the church bench, defeated by destiny, by love, by the very people she once cared about. Giving up happiness was nothing new. It had become a habit. People had already stolen the light from her life — or maybe she had given it away piece by piece, mistaking survival for strength.

She stared at the floor for a long time, contemplating the ruins of herself. The cracks in the stone tiles looked more stable than her own heart. Finally, she raised her head to look at the altar.

“God,” she whispered, her voice barely existing, “you know I am a living corpse. I breathe, I move, I speak… but inside, I have been buried for years.”

She swallowed.

“But in all the chaos of my life, my heart beat for a tiny moment. Just once. A brief breath between disasters. And I came here to give it away. So listen to my letter. No one else should hear it. Just you.”

She closed her eyes and began.

Dear Stranger,

No matter how I think about it, I think I like you. I don’t know when this feeling started. Maybe it was when you showed me kindness. When you asked how I was — are you okay? — and actually meant it.

Among all the people who came to me asking for something first — help, answers, effort, pieces of me — you were the first person who asked if I was okay. No request attached. No expectation behind it. Just a question.

Even if to you it was nothing more than courtesy, to me it was everything. Those were the words I had been dying to hear my whole life. And they came from the most unexpected person.

Childish. Stupid. So foolish.

But that was enough. Just that. My heart beat for a moment. I took a breath I didn’t know I had been holding for years.

Or your warm smile. Or the gentle way you treated me.

I grew up with little kindness, so I get attracted to it. Even the smallest warmth feels like sunlight to someone who has lived in the cold.

I know you have someone. And after the academic mistake I made, I know you probably don’t like me. I don’t expect anything. I will stop my heart and feelings soon. I always do. But at least once, I wanted to say how I feel. Even if only God hears it.

Love has always been cruel to me. I always love the one who cannot love me back. I fall for small things — a smile, a kind word — and I end up hurt, betrayed, neglected, never acknowledged.

That is the punishment I bring on myself.

Death comes in different ways. Mine came in the form of love.

And yet… even with a dead heart and a tired mind, I still fall. It fascinates me. How can something already broken still break again? I feel like a joker in my own tragedy.

Why, God? Killing me once was not enough?

Next time I see him, my broken heart will sink a little deeper. Don’t worry. My mind is awake. I will look away. I will walk away. Being in a place where I am not wanted, yearning for a heart I can’t have… I know exactly how that story ends.

Her voice disappeared into the vast ceiling.

She did not cry. Even tears had abandoned her long ago.

Her body trembled — deep under the skin, like something inside her was unraveling. It felt like standing at the edge of something and trying not to fall.

Her fingers curled into her thighs, nails pressing through the thin fabric — as if physical pain could distract her from the ache spreading inside her chest. One hand slowly moved upward, pressing against her heart, holding it tightly, as though she could stop it from breaking further if she just applied enough force.

“I am giving it back,” she said, though the words resisted her. The tiny moment she had learned to breathe again. She wanted to hold onto it. God, she wanted to cling to it like the last thread keeping her from collapsing completely. Her whole being tightened around that brief heartbeat — that fragile, foolish spark of hope.

But something louder inside her was screaming.
It’s not yours.
It was never yours.

The voice did not shout. It didn’t need to. It cut through her with cold certainty.

The church remained silent.
God remained silent.
The city outside continued breathing.

And she sat there — not healed, not hopeful — still wanting, still aching — while letting it go anyway.
